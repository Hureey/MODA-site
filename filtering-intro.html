Time-Frequency Domain

Filtering

Introduction

When you have a time-frequency representation (TFR) of a signal, such as the wavelet transform or windowed Fourier transform, you may wish to trace and reconstruct the different oscillatory components of the signal. This may be necessary to separate the amplitude modulated [tooltip?] and frequency modulated components. There are many possible approaches to identify the different components in noisy data, and one way is to start with a TFR. From the curve of a certain frequency component in a TFR, it is possible to estimate characteristics such as instantaneous frequency, phase and amplitude - the question is how to trace this curve. In the TFR, there may be multiple peaks at any one time, and it is hard to identify which peak belongs to which component. Some apparent peaks may just be noise. The Filtering & Ridge Extraction app contains two methods for extracting and reconstructing oscillatory modes.

Butterworth filtering uses a Butterworth bandpass filter to extract phase and amplitude information between two frequencies. The Butterworth filter has a flat frequency response compared to other bandpass filters.

If a TFR is constructed well, the oscillatory components of a time series should appear as amplitude peaks, which together form a ridge. Ridge extraction traces this ridge (it traces the oscillation by moving along the line of maximum amplitude) in a specified frequency range. The method used to trace the ridge will not be described here but for details see: D. Iatsenko, P. V. E. McClintock and A. Stefanovska, On the extraction of instantaneous frequencies from ridges in time-frequency representations of signals.
